Case File 01

What is phishing?

Phishing is a message — email, text, call, or chat — designed to trick you into handing over credentials, money, or access by impersonating someone you'd trust.

Email

Phishing

A mass email impersonating a bank, vendor, or internal system, cast wide hoping a small percentage bite.

Targeted

Spear phishing

Personalized to one person or team using real names, projects, or org details — much harder to spot.

Executive

Whaling

Spear phishing aimed at executives or finance staff, often impersonating a CEO requesting an urgent wire transfer.

SMS

Smishing

The same trick delivered by text message — a fake delivery notice or bank alert with a malicious link.

Voice

Vishing

A phone call impersonating IT support, a bank, or a government agency to extract information verbally.

Reply chain

Clone phishing

A near-identical copy of a real email you already received, with the link or attachment swapped for a malicious one.

0 of 6 flags examined
Select a numbered pin on the exhibit to reveal what it tells us.
1Spoofed-looking domain

"yourcompany-support.net" is not your company's real domain — it just contains its name. Attackers register lookalike domains that survive a quick glance.

2Manufactured urgency

Threats of suspension, deadlines, and "final notice" language are designed to make you act before you think. Legitimate IT tickets rarely come with a countdown.

3Generic greeting

"Dear Valued User" instead of your name suggests a mass mailing, not a message from a system that actually knows your account.

4Spelling and grammar slips

"Suspention" and "you're mailbox" are the kind of small errors that a real corporate IT notice — usually templated and proofread — wouldn't contain.

5Disguised link text

The visible words "Verify My Account Now" hide the actual destination URL. Always hover (don't click) to see where a link really points before trusting it.

6Unexpected attachment

A .zip labeled as a "verification form" is a classic malware delivery method. Legitimate identity verification never requires downloading an executable archive.

Case File 03

Fake websites

A phishing email's job is just to get you to click. The real trap is usually the site on the other end, built to look identical to one you already trust.

Read the URL right to left

The part that matters most — the actual domain — sits directly before the first single slash. Everything to the left of it can be faked.

Real
https://accounts.yourbank.com/login
Fake
https://yourbank.com.verify-secure.io/login
Fake
https://yourbank-online.com/login
Fake
https://yourbahk.com/login

The padlock icon isn't a trust seal

HTTPS and the padlock only mean the connection is encrypted — attackers get free certificates too. A padlock tells you no one can eavesdrop on the connection; it says nothing about who owns the site.

  • Typosquatting — domains one letter off from the real one (yourbahk.com), easy to miss at a glance.
  • Subdomain tricks — real-looking brand name placed as a subdomain of an unrelated domain.
  • Pixel-perfect clones — copied logos, layout, and fonts — visual polish is not proof of legitimacy.
  • Missing or broken pages — privacy policy, "about us", or footer links that go nowhere are a tell on hastily-built fake sites.
  • Unusual login requests — being asked for a password on a page you didn't navigate to directly.
Case File 04

Social engineering tactics

Phishing is social engineering delivered by email. The technology is almost incidental — the real exploit is a handful of predictable psychological levers.

Lever 01

Authority

Impersonating a boss, executive, or official body so the request feels risky to question.

"This is the CEO — I need you to purchase gift cards for a client and send me the codes before my flight boards."
Lever 02

Urgency

A deadline or threat that discourages the pause needed to verify.

"Your access will be revoked in 1 hour unless you confirm your credentials now."
Lever 03

Fear

Warning of a consequence — a fine, a security breach, legal trouble — to short-circuit careful thinking.

"We've detected suspicious sign-in attempts on your account from another country."
Lever 04

Familiarity

Referencing real coworkers, projects, or recent events, often scraped from a public profile or a prior breach.

"Following up on the Q3 budget doc you shared — can you re-send it to this address?"
Lever 05

Reciprocity & curiosity

A prize, refund, or intriguing attachment that offers something in exchange for a click.

"You're eligible for a $50 refund from a recent order — claim it here."
Lever 06

Pretexting

A fabricated scenario — an IT ticket, a vendor invoice, an HR request — built to justify an unusual ask.

A caller claiming to be from IT asks you to "confirm" your password to close out a support ticket you never opened.
Case File 05

Real-world patterns

These are the recurring templates behind most reported incidents — the details change, the shape doesn't.

Pattern · Business Email Compromise

The "urgent wire transfer"

An attacker compromises or spoofs an executive's email and asks finance staff to process an unusual, time-sensitive payment or gift card purchase — deliberately routed around normal approval channels. This pattern has cost organizations billions of dollars globally and remains one of the costliest phishing categories precisely because it targets a process, not just a person.

Pattern · Credential Harvesting

The "fake login page"

A cloned sign-in page for a common productivity or file-sharing tool is sent under the guise of a shared document notification. The victim enters real credentials, which are captured instantly and often replayed within minutes.

Pattern · Vendor / Invoice Fraud

The "updated bank details"

A message impersonating a known supplier asks accounts payable to update the bank details on file "before the next invoice." Future legitimate-looking invoices then route funds to the attacker.

Pattern · Delivery / Package Scam

The "missed delivery" text

A smishing text claims a package couldn't be delivered and links to a page requesting a small "redelivery fee," harvesting card details rather than any real shipment.

Case File 06

Best practices

You don't need to catch every technical detail. A handful of habits neutralize almost all of it.

✓
Verify through a second channelGot an urgent request by email? Call the person using a number you already had — not one from the message.
✓
Hover before you clickCheck where a link actually goes before trusting the visible text.
✓
Go to the site yourselfType the known address directly or use a saved bookmark instead of clicking through an email.
✓
Slow down under pressureUrgency is the tactic, not a reason to skip verification — legitimate requests survive a pause.
✓
Use multi-factor authenticationMFA means a stolen password alone usually isn't enough to get in.
✓
Report itForward suspicious messages to your security or IT team — one report can protect the whole organization.
✕
Don't reuse passwordsA credential leaked from one phished site shouldn't unlock every other account.
✕
Don't trust caller ID or display names aloneBoth can be spoofed convincingly — they're not proof of identity.
